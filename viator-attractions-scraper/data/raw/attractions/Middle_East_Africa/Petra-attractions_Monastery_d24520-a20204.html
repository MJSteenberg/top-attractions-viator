Monastery (Ad Deir) Tours and Tickets

32 reviews

Set in the hills above Petra, the Monastery (also known as Ad Deir or al-Deir) is this ancient city’s most recognizable monument after the Treasury. Carved out of sandstone during the 3rd century BC, the Monastery was built as a Nabatean tomb, but takes its name from the crosses etched into its walls during Byzantine times. It’s 148 feet (45 meters) high.

People Also Ask
Q:What is the Monastery (Ad Deir) known for?
A:
The Monastery is the tallest stone facade in Petra, the rock-cut city constructed some 2,000 years ago in southern Jordan by an ancient, desert-dwelling Arab tribe known as the Nabateans. Nicknamed after the monks that may have lived here in Byzantine times, the structure was likely a memorial built for a Nabatean king.
Q:How do you reach the Monastery (Ad Deir)?
A:
Getting to the Monastery involves a short but steep trek. Follow the main trail through the ancient city toward Basin Restaurant. The Ad Deir trail picks up just after the eatery. Be warned, it’s all uphill and mercilessly unshaded. Some travelers ride a donkey to the top, but the condition of the animals makes this practice controversial.
Q:Is the hike to the Monastery (Ad Deir) worth it?
A:
Yes. While the Monastery is more challenging to get to than Petra’s other main monuments, that keeps the crowds at bay. This remote and rugged corner of the ancient city is particularly atmospheric. Sure, reaching the Monastery involves climbing over 800 stone steps, but just think of the “end-of-the-world” views waiting for you.
Q:Which is better, the Monastery (Ad Deir) or the Treasury?
A:
The two monuments are very similar in appearance. While they have almost the same design, the Monastery is far larger, and many travelers consider it even more impressive. That said, the Treasury is far easier to get to, so if you have limited time or energy, don’t feel bad for not seeing both.
Q:Do I need a guide, or can I visit the Monastery (Ad Deir) alone?
A:
The uphill climb toward the Monastery only goes in one direction, making it easy for independent travelers to navigate it independently. But having a local Bedouin guide is recommended. You will be able to branch off the beaten path and also get insights into the region’s history and culture.
Q:What safety precautions are there for visiting the Monastery (Ad Deir)?
A:
Don’t skimp on sun protection. The daytime sun can be uncomfortably powerful year-round; avoid the strongest midday rays if possible. Wear a hat and sturdy shoes. If you’re worried about keeping your balance (the stone steps are uneven), consider taking along hiking poles or a walking stick. And don’t forget to stay hydrated.
