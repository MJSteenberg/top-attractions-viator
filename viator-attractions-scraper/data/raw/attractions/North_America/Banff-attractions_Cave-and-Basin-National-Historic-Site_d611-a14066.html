Cave and Basin National Historic Site Tours and Tickets

The Cave and Basin National Historic Site in Banff National Park is more than just a neat place to explore. The First Nations who discovered the cave consider the hot springs a sacred site. Today, interactive interpretive exhibits make the birthplace of Canada’s national parks system—known today as Parks Canada—a must-visit.

Q:How much does it cost to visit the Cave and Basin National Historic Site in 2024?
A:

Currently, prices vary, costing C$9 for adults and C$7.50 for seniors and commercial groups. Youth can enter for free. Entry comes with the Parks Discover Pass, and you can purchase a combined ticket for the Banff Upper Hot Springs and the Cave and Basin National Historic Site.

Q:What can you see at the Cave and Basin National Historic Site?
A:

Head down the tunnel and into the caves at the Cave and Basin site to see rock formations, the blue pools, a waterfall, and sunlight streaming in from the hole in the top of the caves. There are boardwalks above the cave, too, which provide unique viewpoints.

Q:How much time should I spend at the Cave and Basin National Historic Site?
A:

Plan to spend a few hours thoroughly exploring the Cave and Basin National Historic Site, and make sure to take time to visit the Banff Upper Hot Springs while you’re in the neighborhood. They are just a short drive away and well worth a soak.

Q:Is the Cave and Basin National Historic Site good for kids?
A:

Yes, kids often enjoy exploring the underground cave, and the site offers an educational opportunity to learn about the site’s significance for Canada’s Indigenous peoples, local conservation efforts, and how people and land are connected. Many kids also like to soak in the nearby thermal hot springs.

Q:Is the Cave and Basin National Historic Site worth a visit?
A:

Yes, the Cave and Basin National Historic Site is worth a visit. You can learn about the geography, history, and current cultural relevance when you tour this area, all while taking in spectacular views of the Rocky Mountains. The nearby thermal springs offer a relaxing soak after your visit.
