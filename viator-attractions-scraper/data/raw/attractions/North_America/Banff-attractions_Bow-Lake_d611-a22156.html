Bow Lake Tours and Tickets

Visiting Bow Lake feels like stepping into a postcard—you’ll half-expect to see “CANADA” emblazoned in the sky in bold font. Its water shimmers an icy blue while the snow-capped mountains rise in the background, offering the quintessential Rocky Mountain view. This idyllic place is a perfect picnic destination, and you can take the chance to stretch your legs as you explore the scenic Icefields Parkway.

Q:How much does it cost to visit Bow Lake?
A:

There is no specific entrance fee for Bow Lake itself, as it’s located within Banff National Park. However, visitors must pay a park entrance fee to access the park—as of 2024, that fee is around $11CAD per adult for a day pass, with discounts for seniors and free entry for children under 17.

Q:What are the best activities to do at Bow Lake?
A:

The best activities to do at Bow Lake include hiking the Bow Glacier Falls Trail, a popular, relatively easy hike with excellent views; renting a canoe or kayak and exploring the lake's serene waters in summer; watching for wildlife, like bears, elk, and various bird species; and picnicking in the fabulous scenery.

Q:Is Bow Lake accessible year-round?
A:

Bow Lake is accessible year-round but may vary depending on weather conditions. The area is typically plowed and open during winter, allowing for winter activities like snowshoeing and cross-country skiing. However, some facilities may be closed in winter, and road access can be limited due to snow and ice.

Q:What is the best time of year to visit Bow Lake?
A:

The best time to visit Bow Lake is from late spring to early fall (May to September) when the weather is milder and all facilities and activities are fully operational. Summer is particularly popular for hiking, canoeing, and enjoying the scenery. Fall offers exquisite autumn colors, while winter provides opportunities for snow sports and a quieter experience.

Q:Can I swim in Bow Lake?
A:

Technically, you can swim in Bow Lake, but it’s generally not recommended because it’s so cold, even in summer. The water is glacially fed, so it can be quite chilly (and currents can be stronger than you think). While some brave souls may dip their toes, it's important to be cautious and aware of what you’re literally getting into.
