La Moneda Palace (Palacio de la Moneda) Tours and Tickets

7,184 reviews

The presidential palace known as La Moneda Palace (Palacio de la Moneda)is one of Santiago’s architectural icons. A giant Chilean flag billows before the white, neoclassical building, which houses movie theaters, art galleries, and an independent bookstore. Look for the statue of former president Salvador Allende at the southeast corner of Plaza de la Constitución.

Q:How much does it cost to visit La Moneda Palace in 2025?
A:

Visiting La Moneda Palace is free, but fees apply for tours and exhibitions within the on-site Cultural Center. Many visitors opt to see the palace from the outside, which doesn’t cost a thing. Note that many Santiago tours that stop by the building only view its exterior.

Q:What is the best time of day to visit La Moneda Palace?
A:

You'll need to visit La Moneda Palace on a weekday between late morning and early evening If you want to go inside. There's also a changing of the guard ceremony held on the odd-numbered days of the month in the morning; if joining a tour, check with your guide for details.

Q:What’s the best way to get to La Moneda Palace via public transportation?
A:

The best way to get to La Moneda Palace by public transport is by taking metro Line 1 to La Moneda station, which is a 5-minute walk away. Alternatively, a large number of bus lines stop close by on Av. Alameda Libertador Bernardo O'Higgins.

Q:Are there any nearby museums or landmarks to visit along with La Moneda Palace?
A:

Yes, there are numerous landmarks and museums within a short walking distance of La Moneda Palace. The palatial structure sits right in front of the Plaza de la Constitución, while the beautiful Metropolitan Cathedral (Catedral Metropolitana) and the adjacent Plaza de Armas are only a 10-minute walk away.

Q:Is visiting La Moneda Palace worth it?
A:

Yes, visiting La Moneda Palace is worth it, even if you just stop to admire its exterior. It also has a fascinating history: Although the building was originally constructed to be a mint, it was primarily used as a presidential residence, and it's where dictator Augusto Pinochet staged his coup.
